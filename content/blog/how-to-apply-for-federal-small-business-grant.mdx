Federal grants are real money — and unlike loans or equity, you don't repay them or give up ownership. The federal government awards over $700 billion in grants annually. A meaningful fraction of that is specifically set aside for small businesses.

The catch: the application process is deliberately rigorous. Here's how it actually works.

## What Federal Grants Are (and Aren't)

A federal grant is an award of funds from a government agency to an eligible recipient for a specific purpose — research, workforce development, infrastructure, innovation, public health, and more. You don't pay it back, and you don't give equity.

Grants are not:
- **Loans** — no repayment
- **Contracts** — you're not delivering a service to the government; you're doing work the government wants done in the public interest
- **Free money with no strings** — grants come with reporting requirements, use restrictions, and sometimes matching fund obligations

Most federal grants require that funds be used for their stated purpose. Misuse can result in clawbacks and legal liability.

## Step 1: Find Relevant Grants

**Grants.gov** is the official federal portal listing all federal grant opportunities. You can search by:
- CFDA (Catalog of Federal Domestic Assistance) number
- Funding agency
- Keyword
- Eligibility criteria
- Closing date

For small businesses specifically, also search:
- **SBIR.gov** — SBIR and STTR grants for R&D companies (over $4 billion/year)
- **SBA.gov** — Small Business Administration grants and programs
- **USDA grants** — rural businesses and agriculture
- **EDA grants** — Economic Development Administration, for economic development projects
- **NIH, NSF, DOE** — research grants by agency

## Step 2: Check Your Eligibility

Every federal grant has an eligibility section. Read it carefully before investing time in an application. Common eligibility requirements:

- **Entity type** — some grants are only for nonprofits; others are only for for-profit businesses
- **Business size** — SBA size standards define "small business" differently by NAICS code; verify yours
- **Location** — some grants are state-specific or target rural areas
- **Industry/sector** — most grants target specific fields
- **SAM.gov registration** — almost all federal grants require an active SAM.gov registration

If you don't meet the eligibility requirements, don't apply. The review process will catch it and disqualify your application.

## Step 3: Read the Funding Opportunity Announcement (FOA)

The FOA (also called NOFO — Notice of Funding Opportunity) is the complete document that describes:
- What the grant is for
- Who is eligible
- How much funding is available
- What you must submit
- How applications will be scored
- The deadline

Read the entire FOA before writing a word of your application. Pay particular attention to the **review criteria** — these are the exact factors reviewers will score your application on. Your application should directly and explicitly address each criterion.

## Step 4: Get Your SAM.gov Registration Active

Most federal grants require an active SAM.gov registration before you can submit through Grants.gov. Registration takes 7–10 business days, so start this at least two weeks before any deadline.

See our [complete SAM.gov registration guide](/blog/how-to-register-on-sam-gov) for a step-by-step walkthrough.

## Step 5: Register on Grants.gov

Create an account at Grants.gov and link it to your organization's SAM.gov registration. You'll need your UEI number from SAM.gov.

Download and install the required software (usually Adobe Reader with specific settings) before your deadline. Many applicants lose hours fighting PDF forms on deadline day.

## Step 6: Write Your Application

Federal grant applications typically require:

- **Project narrative** — the core of your application; describes what you'll do, why it matters, and how you'll do it
- **Budget and budget justification** — itemized costs with explanations for each line item
- **Organizational capability statement** — your team's qualifications and past performance
- **Evaluation and measurement plan** — how you'll track and report results
- **Letters of support** — from partners, community organizations, or anchor customers

Address the review criteria directly. Use the same language the FOA uses. Reviewers score against those criteria — if you don't address one explicitly, you'll lose points on it even if your project is excellent.

## Step 7: Submit Early

Grants.gov systems slow significantly as deadlines approach. Submit at least 48 hours before the deadline. If there's a technical error at 11:58pm on the deadline night, you have no recourse.

After submission, Grants.gov sends a confirmation email with a tracking number. Save this. Then wait — federal grant decisions typically take 3–6 months.

## What Happens After You Apply

Applications go through several rounds:
1. **Eligibility check** — automated review for completeness and eligibility
2. **Merit review** — expert panel scores your application against the review criteria
3. **Program officer review** — agency staff review top-scoring applications
4. **Award decision** — agency makes final awards and sends notice letters

If you're not selected, request reviewer feedback if the program offers it. Most do. The feedback is invaluable for your next application.

## The Fastest Path to Federal Funding

The SBIR/STTR program is the most accessible for tech startups. Phase I awards are typically $150,000–$275,000, require no matching funds, and are designed specifically for small businesses. If you're doing any kind of innovation or R&D, this is where to start. See our [SBIR vs STTR comparison](/blog/sbir-vs-sttr-grants) for a full breakdown.
